Burden of disease in West Sussex

The Global Burden of Disease Study (GBD) is one of the most comprehensive worldwide epidemiological collaborations describing mortality and morbidity from major diseases and injuries and risk factors to health at global, national, regional, and local levels. It provides a standardised approach to estimating incidence (new cases) and prevalence (total number of cases) of ill health and death as well as a measure of the number of potential years lost due to disability, ill health and death.

Data are available for every year between 1990 and 2017 and each year new data and methodological innovations are fed into models, revising annual estimates for 360 diseases and injuries and 85 risk factors.

Where possible, local observational data (e.g. mortality and GP records or local studies) are included in the estimates but for a large number of diseases there is simply no recorded information on prevalence. The GBD ethos is that an uncertain estimate, even when data are sparse or not available, is preferable to no estimate at all. As a result, you should consider that there is a margin of error when interpreting these results as they are simply estimates or 'best guesses'.

Concepts

Deaths

In England, vital registers are maintained by the Office for National Statistics (ONS), which record the underlying cause of death for every death. Whilst the GBD study uses these registers for estimates of deaths because there is a complete population coverage, a small proportion of causes of death are re-coded to other causes in line with GBD algorithms identifying non-specific, ill-defined or other causes not considered underlying causes by ONS.

Years of life lost

YLLs are calculated by subtracting the age at death from the longest possible life expectancy for a person at that age. For example, if the longest life expectancy for men in a given country is 75, but a man dies of cancer at 65, this would be 10 years of life lost due to cancer.

Years lived with disability

These are estimates of the number of years lived in less than ideal health. Some conditions such as influenza last for only a few days, whereas epilepsy can last a lifetime. It is measured by taking the prevalence of the condition multiplied by a disability weight which reflects the severity of different conditions and these are developed using a variety of data sources. For England, this includes national surveys, the national cancer registration system, hospital episode statistics and multiple published studies on various disease and condition topics.

Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)

One DALY equals one lost year of healthy life and is the sum of years of life lost and years lived with disability. This is the best measure to consider the overall impact of a specific cause or group of causes on a population.

Standardised values

Looking at crude estimates helps to know what the true impact might be for your area and this can help to plan services (e.g. to anticipate the number of patients a service might need to cater for, the number of cases).

You could compare these estimates with those in other areas, or over time, to see if things are better in some areas or are improving over time although any differences (or similarities) in estimates could be at least in part due the underlying differences in populations (e.g. areas with fewer or greater numbers of older people, or the shear size of the population).

For example area A may have recorded 2,000 deaths and area B recorded 1,500 deaths. You might think that area A has a much bigger issue of mortality than area B. However, if area A has a population of 500,000, the rate of deaths is 400 deaths per 100,000 population. If area B has a population of 300,000, then the rate of deaths is higher in area B (500 per 100,000).

Cause groups:

The GBD 2017 diseases/conditions are split and aggregated into four hierarchical levels. For this analysis we explore levels 1,2, and 3.

Level 1 contains three broad cause groups (communicable, maternal, neonatal and nutirional diseases (CMNN), non-communicable diseases (NCD) and injuries).

These are then split into 22 level 2 cause groups.

There are 169 level 3 diseases/conditions and some of these are further expanded to an additional 165 level 4 diseases/conditions. Level 3 conditions can be explored using the bubble figures on this page or at the bottom in the 'Exploring conditions in more detail' section.
