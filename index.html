Exploring the [global] burden of disease in West Sussex in 2017

The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) is one of the most comprehensive, worldwide, epidemiological collaborations describing life expectancy, as well as mortality and morbidity from major diseases and injuries and risk factors to health at global, national, regional, and local levels. The GBD provides a standardised approach to estimating incidence (new cases) and prevalence (total number of cases) of ill health and death as well as a measure of the number of potential years lost due to disability, ill health and death.

Data are currently available for years between 1990 and 2017 and each year new data and methodological innovations are fed into models, revising annual estimates for 360 diseases and injuries and 85 risk factors.

There is a vast wealth of data available in the GBD, and it is impossible to try to cover all of it in this analysis, which serves to present an overall picture of burden of disease in West Sussex. The data is all publically available from the GBD online results tool; but please contact me if you want some help accessing or exploring particular questions that arise from this preliminary analysis.

Key findings

  • Life expectancy improvements have stalled, whilst healthy life expectancy has decreased meaning that on average we are spending one in every eight years in less than ideal health
  • In 2017, life expectancy at birth was estimated to be 81.7 years, with females having a higher life expectancy (83.5 years) compared to males (79.9 years).
  • Cardiovascular diseases such as stroke, heart attack, and coronary atery disease are the largest causes of death in West Sussex, although cancers (neoplasms) cause the most premature mortality (years of life lost).
  • Non-fatal conditions are accounting for an increasingly large amount of overall burden of ill health.
  • Musculoskeletal conditions such as low back and neck pain are the third largest cause of overall disease burden as measured by Disability Adjusted Life Years lost in West Sussex.
  • The findings from the GBD at a local, regional and national level helps policy makers to allocate resources based on the level of burden and target key risk factors.

Caveats and limitations

The GBD ethos is that an uncertain estimate, even when data are sparse or not available, is preferable to no estimate at all. As a result, you should consider that there is a margin of error when interpreting these results as they are simply estimates or 'best guesses'.

Estimates from the GBD do not compare directly to reported statistics within England (where these are available) as all estimates are constructed using the GBD methodology.

A change in rank does not necessarily mean that a metric for a cause has got better/worse.

Whilst risk factors are presented in three groups, these should not be viewed as mutually exclusive. It is clear that behavioural risks (such as diet) are associated with metabolic risks (such as obesity)

The GBD 2017 diseases/conditions and risks are split and aggregated into hierarchical levels. The cause list is mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive at every level of aggregation; causes not individually specified are captured in residual categories, such as 'other intestinal infectious diseases'.

Is life expectancy improving in West Sussex?

Life expectancy has increased in the last 20 years, from 79 years in 1997 to 81.7 years in 2017 (81.3 years to 83.5 years among females and from 76.5 years to 79.9 years among males). In the last five years, however, overall life expectancy at birth has actually gone down (from 82.2 years in 2012).

Healthy life expectancy has also increased since 1997 from 68.4 years overall (69.5 years and 67 years for females and males respectively) to 69.9 years in 2017 (70.6 years and 69.1 years for females and males respectively). The gain in healthy life expectancy has not been as high (in terms of percentage increase or actual years) compared to gains in overall life expectancy, and similar to overall life expectancy, healthy life expectancy estimates have declined since 2012.

Life expectancy and healthy life expectancy at birth by sex; West Sussex; 1990 - 2017

There are stark differences in life expectancy by sex; with male life expectancy consistently trailing behind female life expectancy. In fact, it took until 2011, for male life expectancy at birth (80.3 years) in West Sussex to reach the life expectancy at birth for females in 1990 (80.1 years), although by this time, female life expectancy at birth was now 83.8 years.

The gap between male and female life expectancy has narrowed over time. In 1990, when estimates start, females could expect to live 5.2 years longer (80.1 years) compared to males who, on average would not live to see their 75th birthdays (74.9 years). In 2007, male life expactancy at birth had risen to 79.3 years and female life expectancy had risen to 83 years (a gap of 3.7 years). The gap has largely stayed around 3.5 years since then.

The gap is consistently narrower between males and females for healthy life expectancy; around 3 years in 1990, dropping to 1.5 years in 2007 and fluctuating around this figure since.

It is possible to combine these two estimates (overall life expectancy and healthy life expectancy) to explore how much time, on average, a person could expect to live in good and poor health and how this changes over time and between males and females. Healthy life expectancy and living with disability and poor health; West Sussex; 1990-2017

A person born in 2017, can expect to live (on average) 69.9 years in good health and 11.9 years living with some level of disability or illness. This equates to 14.5% which is one in seven years spent in less than ideal health. This proportion of time spent with some burden of disease is the highest since 1990 when, a person could expect to live 13.3% of their life (10.3 years) with some disability or illness. The proportion has either remained stable or increased every year since 1990.

Females consistently have a larger gap between healthy life expectancy and overall life expectancy compared to males. Females born in 2017 could expect (on average) to live 15.5% (12.9 years) in less than ideal health whilst males could expect to live 13.5% (10.8 years) in equivalent burden.

So, whilst life expectancy has largely increased over time in West Sussex and male life expectancy has crept closer to female life expectancy, females spend more of their time in poorer health. It appears that the narrowing of the gap between males and females could be explained in part by slightly larger gains in male health expectancies.

Life expectancy

Life expectancy is a measure of the number of years an average person can expect to live at a given age. For this analysis, we explore the life expactancy at birth (up to 1 year old age).

Healthy life expectancy

Healthy life expectancy takes into acount the non-fatal burden of disease and summarises the number of years an average person can expect to live without poor health. The difference between Healthy life expectancy and Life expectancy generally, gives us an idea of the average time people spend in poor health.

In 2017, almost 90% of deaths (8,522 deaths) in West Sussex were caused by non-communicable diseases (these are coloured blue/purple in the figure below). Communicable, maternal, neonatal and nutritional diseases accounted for 7% of deaths (677 deaths) and the majority of these are respiratory infections. Injuries caused 3% (279 deaths).

In West Sussex, cardiovascular diseases account for almost one in every three deaths in 2017 (2,839 deaths). The second largest cause of death was neoplasms (2,752 deaths).

Top ten causes of death (level 2 groups); all ages; West Sussex; 2017

There are a number of differences in the top ten causes of death for males and females. The top three causes of death for females follow the same pattern as for all people combined. For males, neoplasms are the top cause of death followed by cardiovascular diseases and neurological conditions.

Self harm is the 10th biggest level 2 cause of death overall in West Sussex (82 deaths, around 1% of all deaths in the county) and for males specifically (61 deaths). For females, however, self-harm does not appear in the top ten level 2 causes of death and is replaced by musculoskeletal disorders as having a greater impact on mortality in West Sussex estimated to be attributable to 50 deaths in 2017 among females.

Deaths

In England, vital registers are maintained by the Office for National Statistics (ONS), which record the underlying cause of death for every death. Whilst the GBD study uses these registers for estimates of deaths because there is a complete population coverage, a small proportion of causes of death are re-coded to other causes in line with GBD algorithms identifying non-specific, ill-defined or other causes not considered underlying causes by ONS.

Other ways to measure the burden of disease

The leading burdens of disease in West Sussex in 2017 are given in the table below. You'll notice there are actually four top 10s and these reflect the various ways conditions affect our health. Descriptions of each measure of burden are given to the left.

Whilst cardiovascular diseases and neoplasms are the biggest contributors to mortality, some conditions which have fairly low mortality rates can cause significant levels of disability.

For example, musculoskeletal conditions account for 0.7% of deaths in West Sussex in 2017 (70 deaths) but were estimated to be responsible for almost one in four years of life lived with disability (28,852, 23.1%) in West Sussex.

Top ten causes of ill health and mortality; persons; all ages; West Sussex; 2017

The Disability Adjusted Life Years lost (DALY) measure is the best measure of the combined burden of a disease as it includes the impact of premature mortality as well as years of life lived with the burden of a disease. Whilst the number of deaths in West Sussex was 9,478, if you break down this mortality to the number of premature years potentially lost, the burden exceeds 126,700 years.

Importantly, the number of years of life lost (premature death) is comparable with the total estimated number of years people in West Sussex spend living with disability. This indicates that in West Sussex, people are living as much of their life in less than ideal health as they are losing potential years of their life outright. Obviously the level of disability experienced by individuals will vary from minimal interruption to significant disruption of daily life and dependence on others for care.

We can see from this measure that neoplasms have the biggest overall impact on health in West Sussex accounting for one in five DALYs, followed by cardiovascular diseases (about one in six DALYs). More than one in every 10 DALYs lost to people in West Sussex (29,655) and musculoskeletal disorders.

Years of life lost (YLLs)

YLLs are calculated by subtracting the age at death from the longest possible life expectancy for a person at that age. For example, if the longest life expectancy for men in a given country is 75, but a man dies of cancer at 65, this would be 10 years of life lost due to cancer.

Years lived with disability (YLDs)

These are estimates of the number of years lived in less than ideal health. Some conditions such as influenza last for only a few days, whereas epilepsy can last a lifetime. It is measured by taking the prevalence of the condition multiplied by a disability weight which reflects the severity of different conditions and these are developed using a variety of data sources. For England, this includes national surveys, the national cancer registration system, hospital episode statistics and multiple published studies on various disease and condition topics.

Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)

One DALY equals one lost year of healthy life and is the sum of years of life lost and years lived with disability. This is the best measure to consider the overall impact of a specific cause or group of causes on a population.

The figure illustrates that at this level, ischemic heart disease is the biggest cause of death in West Sussex (1,292 deaths), followed by Alzheimer's disease and other dementias (1,146 deaths), stroke (754 deaths), lower respiratory infections (617 deaths) and chronic obstructure pulmonary disease (COPD, 530 deaths). The same conditions make up the top five causes of deaths for males although for females, Alzheimer's and other dementias are the biggest cause of death (770 deaths) followed by heart disease (557 deaths).

Ischemic heart disease is also the largest contributor to premature mortality estimated to cause 15,731 years of life lost, which is almost double the next biggest cause of premature mortality (Alzheimer's disease and other dementias; 8,672 YLLs).

For females, Alzheimer's and other dementias and heart disease are the top two causes of premature mortality although of note is that cancers of the breast and lung and trachael cancers as well as stroke all contribute comparable burden on premature mortality.

Exploring the non-fatal burden of disease (years of life lived with disability) shows that low back pain is the biggest cause of disability (16,066 YLDs), followed by headache disorders (7,422 YLDs), depressive disorders (6,052 YLDs), neck pain (5,978 YLDs) and hearing loss (including age-related hearing loss; 5,041 YLDs). Of note, diabetes and falls contribute comparable levels of burden (4,887 and 4,877 years lived with disability) but just miss out on the top five conditions. Also of note is that neoplams, in comparison, contribute little to years lived with disability.

There are some interesting differences among male and female causes of YLDs. For females, the same top four contions cause the most disability burden, although COPD replaces hearing loss as the fifth biggest cause of YLDs. For males, low back pain and hearing loss are the top two conditions for YLDs, although diabetes is the third biggest cause of disability, followed by headache disorders and neck pain.

When premature mortality and disability are considered together, ischemic heart disease is the biggest cause of burden with 16,611 disability adjusted life years lost in West Sussex, in 2017. This is followed closely by low back pain with 16,066 years lost.

Two thirds of DALYs lost as a result of ischemic heart disease in 2017 were among males (11,033 DALYs) and for males is the biggest cause of burden by a considerable margin. Conversely, 44% (7,038 DALYs) of the overall burden of low back pain was among males.

How does ill health affect people of different ages?

Some conditions affect people at particular stages in their lives. Thankfully, a large proportion of deaths happen after the age of 75 in West Sussex (73%, 6,907 deaths in 2017). However, the non-fatal burden of disease can happen much earlier in the life course; with disability concentrated among those of working age.

Standardised values

Looking at crude estimates helps to know what the true impact might be for our area (e.g. to anticipate the number of patients a service might need to cater for, the number of cases) and this can help to plan services.

You could compare these crude estimates with those in other areas, or over time, to see if things are better in some areas or are improving over time, although any differences (or similarities) in estimates could be at least in part due the underlying differences in populations (e.g. areas with fewer or greater numbers of older people, or the shear size of the population).

As an example, area A may have recorded 2,000 deaths and area B recorded 1,500 deaths. You might think that area A has a much bigger issue of mortality than area B. However, if area A has a population of 500,000, the rate of deaths would be 400 deaths per 100,000 population. If area B has a population of 300,000, then the rate of deaths is higher in area B (500 per 100,000 population).

Risk factor

A risk factor is defined as an attribute, behavior, or exposure, which is causally associated with an increased (or decreased) probability of a disease or injury. Risk factors can be protective if the probability of ill health decreases. It should be noted that each risk factor may be directly or indirectly associated with an outcome and risk factor contribution to ill health is not mutually exclusive.

Furthermore, a combination of risk factors may jointly influence outcomes and there is often overlap of multiple risk factors/exposures. As such, it is not possible to sum the contribution of two or more risk factors (i.e smoking and drinking alcohol) to say these two risk factors cause a certain proportion of the total burden of a condition.

For example, there were estimated to be 2,752 deaths caused by neoplasms in West Sussex in 2017. Of those deaths, 43% (1,190 deaths) are attributed to atleast one risk factor in the GBD study. We are able to say that Tobacco use for example, is attributable to 567 deaths as a result of neoplasms, occupational risks were attributable to neoplasm 258 deaths and that alcohol use was attributable to 188. We are not able, however, to say that the three risk factors combined account for 1,013 deaths because we do not know the overlapping risk contribution at this level of detail.

Overlap in risk contribution is available for level 1 risks (environmental, behavioural and metabolic risks).
